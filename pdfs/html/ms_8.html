Notes

1. The top of the roll is badly damaged: the first few lines are faded, and there are holes in the parchment. Examination under UV light has allowed restitution of much of the faded material with a high degree of confidence, with reference to the closely similar prologues found in MSS A and G. Holes in the parchment have been indicated in my transcription as gaps. [TGH]

2. The topics announced in this prologue are not all included in the manuscript (e.g. fishing and tree names). Coupled with two occasions where the script suddenly becomes smaller (lines 124 and 326), this suggests that cuts were made in what was copied in order to fit the text onto the parchment available. [TGH]

3. The end-line gloss here is too faded to be legible. [TGH]

4. The end-line gloss here is too faded to be legible. [TGH]

5. The deeply faded end-line gloss here is lost to manuscript damage. [TGH]

6. The gloss to pauper is too faded to be clearly legible. Possibly (with a very low degree of certainty) lide? [TGH]

7. The gloss here looks like a variant form of breu, which is an eyebrow, not (as cil means) an eyelash. [TGH]

8. What appears to be a full-line gloss over three lines in the right-hand margin is too faded to read clearly. There seem to be five words; the second may be þirl, the third is and and the fourth may be a []

9. Small cross in the right margin in black ink. [ETFM]

10. Gloss over three lines is too faded to read, but probably translates this line in full. [TGH]

11. I have not been able to interpret the interlinear gloss on this line. [TGH]

12. The first word of the endline gloss, which has had a 't' added into it by the second glossing hand (black ink), is badly faded. It is therefore impossible to be certain which part of the line it is glossing. The rest of the gloss reads þe hand. [TGH]

13. A small marginal cross in black ink against this line. [ETFM]

14. From here on, the scribe writes smaller - perhaps having realised that more lines to a page would be needed? [TGH]

15. The galle or gall bladder is a distinct organ from the spleen, but since the two were closely associated in Galenic humoral theory, I think it likely that the gloss is intended to relate to esplen in the French text. [TGH]

16. The interlinear gloss to reynoun (in the second, black, glossing ink) is illegible – possibly due to erasure? One assumes an original reading along the lines of kidenere. [TGH]

17. ME gloss could be understood as either article the (with de as a variant form) plus noun stert (stalk), or as the verb destert (remove the stalk); I have opted for the latter, though it is not attested in MED (but prefix de– is listed: with depreciative or privitive force). [TGH]

18. This word form is unclear, with several ambiguous letter forms: s/f, u/n and c/t. My tentative reading is asence, with a sense of the fox's cunning or cleverness in trapping its prey, relating to the AND's definitions of to advise; to guide, lead; to instruct, teach. [TGH]

19. The gloss on this line is very faded but appears to repeat the gloss Rakes him visible two lines higher up, at line 222. The occurence at 222 is underlined, suggesting excision by subpunction, with the gloss above it, ganes, written in as a correction (and making far more sense in relation to the line). Accordingly, I have placed rakes hym here, where it corresponds to the French s'espreche. [TGH]

20. Gloss rede appears misplaced on this line, unless it is understood as a key to the entire section. [TGH]

21. Another small black cross in the margin, marking this line - or the whole 'red knight' passage? [ETFM]

22. I have interpreted the superscript addition of e [TGH]

23. One could read this as frauncé or Fraunce. I have preferred the former reading, since the AND entry for paille makes clear that this was a common form in Anglo-French. [TGH]

24. This rubric is incorrect - it belongs with the baking section beginning on line 276. [TGH]

25. The form messuer appears to be an misreading for messner. [TGH]

26. Lines omitted here (compared with other manuscripts at this point) produce a grammatical inconsistency between lines 310 (where the housewife is mentioned in the third person) and 311 (where she is addressed directly). [TGH]

27. As at line 124, the scribe appears to have recalculated the lines/page needed to complete the text, and reduced the spacing between lines (which had gradually increased) from this point. [TGH]

28. Possible influence of the English equivalent windel on this word form? Or simply a scribal misreading of n for u in an exemplar? (Other Tretiz MSS have the form wauder, and the form as found here is unattested.) See also line 342. [TGH]

29. See note to line 340. [TGH]

30. Two possible readings here: (1) d'eus: each of them with another alongside; or (2) deus: each one with two others alongside. [TGH]
