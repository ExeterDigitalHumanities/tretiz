Notes

1. The gloss is unclear. One would expect a nominal form (probably fulmard, based on the other MSS), but illegued looks verbal, possibly the past tense form of leggen (to speak against). In other Tretiz MSS, the equivalent line reads E maudit soit li mau putois; is it possible that illegued corresponded in an exemplar to maudit (with a sense along the lines of accused, criticised) but was erroneously copied across and ascribed to putois within the line as reworked here? [TGH]

2. Two forms of this word are recorded in MSS of the Tretiz and related texts: avaluer (sometimes shortened to valuer) and vauner. AND has an attestation for each of these. Modern French preserves avaloir (see TLF: pièce du harnais qui descend derrière les cuisses des chevaux et les entoure à leur partie inférieure), attested already in the 13th century (Godefroy Compl. 8:245). This suggests that (a)valuer is the more etymologically sound of the two alternatives ('aval'= underneath + '-oir' suffix, a rope passing underneath/round the back of the horse), with vauner possibly derived from misreading of the 'u' as an 'n' and palatalisation of the 'l'. [TGH]

3. The equivalent Latin term is soliva, glossed as sulle and solive in MSS of John of Garland’s Dictionarius (see DMLBS, soliva: beam, rafter, sill. Some Tretiz witnesses (e.g. PT) give the word solive here; others (including GSY) have seilloun. The former word is doubtless derived from the Latin etymon; the latter appears to be the same term as the Dictionarius gloss sulle and is presumably to be related to English sil(le). We have chosen to link to AND headwords solive and seilloun respectively, depending on each manuscript’s reading. [TGH]

4. The gloss is puzzling; the first element is surely dore but the second is less clear. Possibly a corruption of dor crok, ?a hinge or part of a hinge? [TGH]
