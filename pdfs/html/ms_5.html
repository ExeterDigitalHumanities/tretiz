Notes

1. In the upper margin, a faded (later?) hand has written: Nota bene / Un femme [TGH]

2. An interesting alteration in the addressee, from soer as found in most MSS to sire here. [TGH]

3. enpalluer appears to have been initially written as two words (en and palluer, which were then merged into one by extending the join on the n. [ETFM]

4. The form regarez for regar[d]z is dubious. [TGH]

5. The scribe has merged two lines, breaking the couplet rhymes in the process. Compare G 44-46: Mes vostre eel est chaciouse. / Des eus oustés la chacie / E de nes le rupie. []

6. The MS reading deuour is a non-sense; I have corrected following other MSS, which are consistent on this line. [TGH]

7. Other manuscripts of Tretiz agree on the reading croule here, suggesting that aroule is a mistranscription. [TGH]

8. Lines 235 to 240 offer alternative versions of the descriptions of dogs and polecats, presumably drawn from different exemplars. In numbering them, I have followed marginal indications in the MS that line 238 (which appears below line 239 on the page) should follow directly from line 237. [TGH]

9. bayilli is an interesting reading: other Tretiz MSS have the word failli here, defined by AND as inadequate person, failure. [TGH]

10. The infintive form espeluper added by the scribe makes more grammatical sense than past participle espeluké. []

11. AND has only one current attestation for fenerez, with the sense for cutting hay. [TGH]

12. Apparent influence of Latin on this word form. See the note preceding line 835. [TGH]

13. In other MSS, this line ends with the word fusil; in this MS, the verb appears not to have an object. [TGH]

14. The preceding lines would make more sense if re-ordered as follows: 404-5, 408-9, 406-7. This would bring it in line with the version of other MSS, where the subject of dit in 406 is Lady Hue, not mentioned until 408 here. [TGH]

15. FEW, AND and DEAF all record this word form as frenole, a word for which the Tretiz would offer the sole attestation (here and in the section on weather). The scribe here has used the variant u/v form based on w graphie, so there is no doubt about the intended spelling. Given the absence of further attestations, and uncertainty about reading frenole further on (see note to line 555), I have accepted the manuscript reading here. [TGH]

16. AND2 (following Owen) has this as tutoise with the sense of to use 'tu' to someone. Alternatives are possible: given the occasional confusion of t/c forms throughout this copy, could this be a corruption of curtoise? Or a variant of tut/eisé, with the sense of rich, well-to-do? Either of those would make more sense within the syntax of the sentence. [TGH]

17. betnaye is dubious - other Tretiz manuscripts have some form of betumei. []

18. This line is badly garbled. Other manuscripts have variants on: Ou petite rey ne troeve grace (from MS Y) - i.e., you use a nace where a rey is no good. []

19. nace would make more sense in light of the preceding lines; the influence of Middle English net on the reading here seems probable. [TGH]

20. Another corrupted line. MS Y has: Mais plus larges partuz assez / Ad le crevere, i.e. the crevere has larger holes than the nace. The reading here has mistaken partuz as partut; I cannot make sense of the first half of the line. []

21. In the right margin, a later (16th-century?) hand has added this short poem in English, whose wordplay and content (body parts and geography) appear inspired by the tenor of the Tretiz. []

22. I have not been able to identify this word, which (alongside a broader difficulty in making sense of the line) raises the suspicion of a corrupt reading. [TGH]

23. This line is badly garbled. [TGH]

24. It is unclear why a gap was left here, where other MSS would lead one to expect the word aunfe. Is this part of Dove's teaching method, challenging his reader/student to supply the missing word? (See the treatment of the riddle below at line 543 for comparison.) [TGH]

25. The answer to the riddle has deliberately been omitted - presumably as part of the teaching strategy? [TGH]

26. AND has this as frenole (a term for which the Tretiz would offer the sole attestation; see note to line 420), with a definition here of object of little worth. However, a reading of frevole is worth considering. A number of Tretiz manuscripts (including ACPT) give an alternative version of the line Ke par bost de frivole, with frivole glossed as ydel words. [TGH]

27. These two forms, both deriving from the Latin etymon solsequia, have separate headwords in the AND. []

28. The second line of this couplet, as found in other manuscripts, has been omitted; in G, it is En ceo boys deivent crestre. [TGH]

29. I have found no tree type with this name; likely a transcription error for fresne, as in other Tretiz manuscripts. [TGH]

30. An interesting variant; in other manuscripts, it is a king that gives Aliénor her name, rather than a fairy. [TGH]

31. It is difficult to assign a meaningful sense to this line. It appears to be a corrupted version of the line found just over the page, line 644: De su[e]th l'em fet seueus', concerning a dish made with elderflower (the elder tree is mentioned here in the following line here). [TGH]

32. The short length and lack of rhyme in this line suggest that the exemplar's version may originally have read: Ou le russenole, þe nichtingale, as in MSS AbGT, with the English half of the line left out here, perhaps understood as a gloss rather than an integral part of the rhymed text. [TGH]

33. This line is badly garbled; the original reading appears to be: 'Des eiles nou[ent] en eve les pesszons'. [TGH]

34. The word iver, found in other manuscripts but omitted here, appears necessary to make sense of the line. [TGH]

35. In the absence of attestations outside the Tretiz tradition, this insect name might be read as palevole or palenole. I have chosen the former given the semantic relevance of the verb voler [TGH]

36. The river Nene in the East of England? [TGH]

37. Variant spelling of fes. [TGH]

38. Missing line: Entre le chartil e lé moyeaus (from G). [TGH]

39. Compare other MSS: En les limons veit limoner (from G); some possible corruption of limoner here, though it is worth noting that the form limour is used consistently by this scribe (e.g. in line 797); cf. also the form lymeour in Femina. [TGH]

40. The abbreviation mark on un appears otiose. [TGH]

41. Variant spelling of torchaz. [TGH]

42. Another unnecessary macron. [TGH]

43. Latin word form, rather than French - see the note to line 394. [TGH]
