Notes

1. Rothwell's edition of MS G reasonably corrects to En here. In keeping with the desire of the present edition to represent as far as possible the autonomy of scribal interpretation in each copy, where this appears defensible, I have retained the MS reading, understanding the following sense: When he reaches the age where he can understand language (in general) and French (in particular), you should tell him how he should first describe his body. However, instances of erroneous E for En occuring elsewhere in the MS have been emended (see lines 38, 120, 306, 322, 839 and 925). [TGH]

2. The gloss does not fit the French lemma, which means win the ram. [TGH]

3. Ambiguity here as to whether to read Frauncze or fraunczé. Cf. lines 281 and 637. [TGH]

4. As with line 120, here one could read either frauncé or Fraunce. See also line 637. [TGH]

5. The English gloss suggests that this term is a verb (with possible sense of hunting out of season?), but it is listed in AND (with this attestation) only as a noun. [TGH]

6. I agree with AND, and diverge from Rothwell's edition, in viewing this word as distinct from travail - the spelling as trauil is supported by the majority reading trahuil/trahuyl found in MSS BCPTY7. [TGH]

7. Rather oddly, the French lemma appears to have been glossed with an identical French infinitive. If so, this is the only instance of a French gloss in this manuscript. [TGH]

8. The gloss here could be understood as a variant or (as Rothwell's edition treats it) aberrant form of rite: A formal religious act or service, ritual, ceremony. Or it may relate somehow to the obscure word riste, defined by MED as a kind of ale barrel from a single attestation in a 1319 Compotus Roll for Pershore Manor: De lj barellis Cervisie qui vocantur ristes. [TGH]

9. Rothwell (2009) reads the gloss as szyne, which is entirely plausible. I have preferred to read szyve in keeping with line 526, where the relevant minims look more clearly like a v than an n, but it is impossible to decide with certainty between the two readings. [TGH]

10. The scribe of G has an occasional tendency to render en as e (cf. lines G 38, 120, 306, 322, 839 and 925). Given that other manuscripts give the form enruez here, this appears to be one such instance; in which case, and in the absence of corroboration elsewhere, the form eruez may be suspect. [TGH]

11. There is significant corruption in this section - see corrections in-text and accompanying notes. [TGH]

12. Rothwell's 1990 edition has this as frenole, but his 2009 edition corrects to frivole. The glossator of MS G appears to have understood the former reading. [TGH]

13. Unusual (possibly aberrent?) spelling of place. [TGH]

14. A significant ambiguity here, as previously on lines 120 and 281: the manuscript reading could be resolved as either Fraunce (in France) or frauncé (in French). [TGH]

15. Rothwell's edition has this line as Une herbe qe severouse red[i]rez, with the sense: A herb that you will call severouse, and requiring an emendation to the verb to make it make sense. Based on the same rationale, FEW and AND both list severouse as an unidentified plant, with this line (present only in G) as the sole attestation. Our reading allows the verb form to stand as it is in the manuscript, with the sense: A herb that gets the feverish back on their feet referring back to cerlaunge in the preceding line. [TGH]

16. Rothwell (2009) reads nint here, which is plausible; the placement of the flick on top of the i, however, suggests nuit. [TGH]

17. Rothwell's edition misreads the final gloss as iv, with a note commenting: i.e. 'ivy'. But the yf is in fact a yew, and the gloss must be understood as a monogrammatic form of that word (MED does not have it, but it does list the similarly compressed form u. []

18. The morphology of the gloss appears to tag a French third person plural ending (-ent) onto an English verb form stem. [TGH]

20. Other MSS have rivere here, which would make better sense of the line than MS G's reading of yvere. [TGH]

21. The word bureles, written below the line in the red glossing ink, has been crossed out with black ink. It would appear to offer an alternative translation of rais; the same word appears as the gloss to this line in MSS CSTY. To add to the confusion, szlakes here is a unicum, whose precise sense is unclear. [TGH]

23. The same gloss for the wordform fue/feu on this and the preceding line; it is crossed out here – but it is the gloss on 1011, not this one, that is incorrect. [TGH]

28. Other MSS which include this line have the more recognisable form eliz. [TGH]
