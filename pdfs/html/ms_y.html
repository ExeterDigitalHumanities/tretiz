Notes

1. The punctuation in this line departs from Rothwell on account of the presence of an additional 'qe' not found in Rothwell. [ETFM]

2. The gloss to this line appears to be incorrect: in theory, 'hommes' should be a gloss for 'garrettes' in l. 139. One possibility is that, as in G, 'la' was originally a repetition os 'les garrettes', but the gloss was mistakenly carried over when the line was changed. [ETFM]

3. Qi / qe confusion [ETFM]

4. Most likely treseus as a variant of troseus, not tresteus—> trestel as Rothwell suggests for this line in MS T. See AND trusse, definition 2: truss (of hay etc.), and the note to line 561 below. [TGH]

5. An unusual gloss, suggesting perhaps that the scribe is glossing after having completed the text. [ETFM]

6. There is no MED entry for skutchen. OED's earliest attestation of scutch, v.2 – to dress (fibrous material, flax, hemp, cotton, silk, wool) by beating – is from 1733. [TGH]

7. The MED entry for hond(e includes the compound form hond moule under definition 8(j). [TGH]

8. Marginal rubrics partially visible: [...]etz [?] [...]iere. [ETFM]

9. An unusual addition here, namely in the form of two pairs of words: aschappelt[re] / aschappelen and >kormer / kormes. Likely references to tree-names and their relevant fruits. Cf. AND cormer, service-tree; however, Rawlinson G.96 has corna glossed as eglentes, huppe, as does one Dictionarius MS. Aschappeltree is possibly ash-tree, or rose-hip tree misunderstood as a schappel-tree. Likely in the same hand that wrote the marginal rubrics (trimmed) on the previous folio. [ETFM]

10. An unusual choice here, since gal is not attested in AND, with the possible exception of gailler to croak, or gal (to get pleasure). Likely a corruption of Bibbesworth's original use of the English in the line itself. [ETFM]

11. A hitherto-unattested variant spelling. [ETFM]

12. Rothwell corrects this word in both MSS G and T to trestel, but concedes that no equivalent use of that word (to mean a stack of hay) is attested in Godefroy or Tobler-Lommatsch. Definition 4 under trestel in AND contains only variants of this line from the Tretiz and from the closely-derived Nominale sive Verbale, none of which supply the internal 't' that Rothwell adds here (the forms attested are tresel from MS G, tresselle from the Nominale, and renceaus from O). AND also has a hypothetical headword [tasel] for what appears to be the same word used in MS T, line 272 of our edition. In both instances, a far more likely fit is provided by AND trusse (definition 2: truss of hay, etc.), with diminutive trussel or tresel. [TGH]

13. This gloss has been executed on the following line, likely in error (where it would necessarily refer to the entire phrase beginning une femme qe est demuree). [ETFM]

14. The equivalent Latin term is soliva, glossed as sulle and solive in MSS of John of Garland’s Dictionarius (see DMLBS, soliva: beam, rafter, sill. Some Tretiz witnesses (e.g. PT) give the word solive here; others (including GY) have seilloun. We have chosen to link to AND headwords solive and seilloun respectively, depending on each manuscript’s reading. The word seilloun appears to be the same term as the Dictionarius gloss sulle and is presumably to be related to MED sil(le), rather than AND seillun (selion, furrow). [TGH]

15. This is likely a misreading of grace chouz (G 1074). [ETFM]

16. Another instance of a strange gloss here, possibly over-literal (and also found in MS C). [ETFM]
