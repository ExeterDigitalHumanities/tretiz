15. Rothwell corrects the scribal tenail to terail in his edition of MS G. AND2 has both tenail and terrail as forms for balk, ridge of unploughed land, with the Tretiz (MS O) as the only attestation for the former form. We have retained the manuscript form in each case, which suggests a predominance of tenail over terail. [ETFM]

16. The glossator has taken the wrong sense of grele, taking it to mean small as in the previous line. []

17. The AND entry triceler is based on this sole attestation in MS B; AND also has a separate entry for cricele with a cognate meteorological sense, attested in the Nominale sive verbale, with a note signalling the possibility of reading tricele; one presumes that a Tretiz manuscript including this line is the Nominale's source. The similarities of scribal 'c' and 't' makes either reading plausible in both cases. [TGH]

Fukui (1998: 301) reads it as couune and corrects speculatively to coucou. 27. Two forms of this word are recorded by AND from MSS of the Tretiz and related texts: avaluer (sometimes shortened to valuer) and vauner. AND has an attestation for each of these. The minims here could be read as wauner or wauuer as a palatalized form of waluer. Modern French preserves avaloir (see TLF: pièce du harnais qui descend derrière les cuisses des chevaux et les entoure à leur partie inférieure), attested already in the 13th century (Godefroy Compl. 8:245). This suggests that (a)valuer is the more etymologically sound of the two alternatives ('aval'= underneath + '-oir' suffix, a rope passing underneath/round the back of the horse), with vauner possibly derived from misreading of the 'u' as an 'n' and palatalisation of the 'l'. [TGH]
