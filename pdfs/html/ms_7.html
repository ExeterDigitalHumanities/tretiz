La proprete de Fraunceys

[Introduction (Letter)]

Chere sour, pur ceo que vous moy priastes que jeo meyse en escrit pur vo enfaunz aucune chose de aprise en fraunceis en breve paroles, jeo le ay fet solun ceo que les paroles me vynent en memorie que les enfaunz pussent saver les propretés des choses et qaunt deivent dire 'moun' e 'ma', 'toun' e 'ta', 'soun' e 'sa' , 'le' e 'la', 'moy' e 'je' .

[Harvesting corn in the fields]

Aloms as preez e as chaunps
De aprendre fraunceis a nous enfanz.
264De fauz fauchez un aundoyne de pree.
De faucyl siez un javele de blee.
Les gaveles en garbes lyez.
En treseus les garbes metez.
268Un warrot de peys alacez[27].
Lé favaz de feves de ceo lyez.
Kaunt tens est de karier
Vos chars fatez lors charger.
272Ses chyvals deyt ly charetter
De sa ryorte guyer.
En la graunge vous bleez vuyez,
Dehors la grance vous bleez tassez.
276Une meye est en grange

Moylon appelez ceo que est de feyn
E tas ceo que est de greyn.

Notes

1. The Latin gloss to greve here and on the following line may relate to the verb descindo, to divide (see the entry for this term in Lewis and Short). [TGH]

2. The Latin gloss appears to contain an error: according to Lewis and Short and DMLBS, lucta corresponds to wrestling, while luctus translates as sorrow, lamentation, mourning. [TGH]

3. The gloss appears to be schar (the pubic region, the groin), which would be incorrect here. (The same word form appears, as the correct gloss to penuyl, on line 115.) [TGH]

4. THe gloss is hard to read (and, because of its proximity to the binding, must have been hard to write); I offer a tentative, but plausible, resolution of the abbreviations. [TGH]

5. I have interpreted the gloss here as an English loanword from Latin, but it could equally be taken to be Latin. The MED has this gloss as the earliest attestation of the word in English, with the next earliest (1398) coming from Trevisa's translation of De proprietatibus rerum. [TGH]

6. The gloss to blaçouns (shoulder-blade) is puzzling, since Lewis and Short defines lesiones (under laesio) as (act of) injuring, harming or injury, harm; wound. Perhaps the glossator was confusing the French lemma with the similar-looking word blesours? []

7. The scribe has originally written deuz (an error in the exemplar?) but realised that the correct reading should be dé. [TGH]

8. This is one of two commentary glosses in this manuscript (the other is at line 216). It offers not a translation of any of the terms in the line but a clarification of the subject. I have treated this as English and resolved the abbreviation accordingly as a possessive form, but it could quite plausibly be read as Latin, or even as French. [TGH]

9. The relationship between gloss and lemma is obscure, and may bear witness to the glossator's difficulties in understanding the line. Has venteresce been interpreted as some kind of entrance? [TGH]

10. The glosses are reversed compared with what one would expect from the gender of the articles: char (flesh) is usually feminine and char (cart) is masculine. [TGH]

11. The relationship between the lemma ensechisent (AND: dry up or wither) and the gloss conjacent (Lewis and Short, under coniaceo: lie together; DMLBS, under conjacere: to be adjacent) is unclear, and may reveal the glossator's misunderstanding of the line. []

12. The gloss to derechef is very difficult to read. With a high degree of confidence, the first three letters can be transcribed as ter, possibly a variant form of ther- as prefix, but the remainder of the word is uncertain and I therefore refrain from hypothesising a definite meaning. [TGH]

13. Read alongside other manuscripts, it appears this rendering is a corruption of original Au dyner (at dinnertime). The gloss seems to attempt to make sense of a French word-form that approximates the verbarraier (to prepare, make ready), offering the cognate English translation araye. The implied sense would be something like: Give him eggs to make ready / And prepare them to his taste. An alternative reading of the French might be as A royner – from either runger (to gnaw) or just possibly roigner(to scour, scrape) – since the vowel preceding the y appears to have been overwritten and is unclear; but in this case it is harder to make sense of the English gloss. [TGH]

14. The glosses to entrut here and germinoun on line 170 are inverted compared to the other manuscripts, and to the relevant dictionary entries for stren (the germinal vesicle in the yolk of an egg) and skin (an internal membrane). [TGH]

15. The glosses to entrut on line 167 and germinoun here are inverted compared to the other manuscripts, and to the relevant dictionary entries for stren (the germinal vesicle in the yolk of an egg) and skin (an internal membrane). [TGH]

16. As with the parts of the egg above (lines 167 and 170), the gloss here is in disaccord with the relevant AND and MED definitions and the evidence of the other manuscripts, in which the glosses stalk and stert have the sense the stalk or stem of a fruit. In other manuscripts, the word core is often found as the gloss to pepinere (line 176 below). [TGH]

17. nide is attested in English by the OED from 1679, either from French nid or Latin nidus. Given the temporal distance between the manuscript and this earliest OED attestation, we prefer to view the gloss here as Latin rather than English. [TGH]

18. The gloss storkes is incorrect - there is likely confusion with stare. [TGH]

19. Placement suggests that the gloss here corresponds to the end-line verb groule; but since the only sense I can make of the gloss is as the noun heroun, I have chosen to link it to the preceding grue; this does, however, force one to assume that the glossator has erred in the choice of bird-name to translate the French. [TGH]

20. The Latin gloss here appears to summarize the implication of the French line: the hen's eggs might come from any place [TGH]

21. The gloss to veyler, how, offers a sense of hoven not currently attested in the MED, which does however record adjacent senses such as to remain, stay, linger and wait around, hang about. [TGH]

22. The English gloss mistakith appears not to correspond to the French term bayle, and is hard to explain. [TGH]

23. The gloss, apparently with the sense of to spy; to look for; to find out, discover, makes little sense in relation to the lemma espreche, which the AND defines as to stretch (drawing on the Tretiz tradition, where the only attestations for this word are found). There is clearly obscurity here. Could the glossator have misread it as a form of the verb esprendre, to learn, find out? [TGH]

24. Again, there is obscurity about the connection between the lemma (besche, a tool for digging) and the gloss. The latter could be read as English a cantel (some kind of digging tool, related in shape to the senses listed in MED - the rim of a shield; a chunk, piece, slice; a stroke in fencing; or a piece of timber used as a brace or support), English a cautel (a precaution), or French a cautel (with precaution; or through a trick, machination); none of these bear an obvious relation to the lemma. Moreover, all three options would make this gloss unique for this manuscript: the two English renderings would make this the only noun gloss which includes an article; if read as French, this would be the only French-language gloss in MS 7. [TGH]

25. A previously-unattested spelling of the gloss-word, with possible interference from abaishen (cf. line 214 above). [TGH]

26. The second occurence of flaterer, as the gloss to flater, appears to be an error for the verb form flateren. [TGH]

27. Comparison with other manuscripts might suggest that alacez is an error for aracez, but assuming that a sense of to enlace, lace up (anticipating lyez in the next line) is understood here, the reading can be allowed to stand. []

28. I have not found a way to make sense of jeo in the manuscript here. My understanding of this difficult passage, as found across the tradition, is: If you bring people together for a party, make sure you invite those who are under investigation for bad deeds, who would be very quick to cause trouble and harm. But do not be so ill-advised as to give them rancid/leftover meat. []

29. Nota in the form of a cross next to this line. [TGH]

30. In other manuscripts this line is a negation (If we don't have success there, let us go (instead) to the lake), which appears more logical. [TGH]

31. At this point MS 7 breaks with the order of topics found in the other manuscripts, jumping ahead to the Hosting a meal section; once it reaches the end of the text, it jumps back to the sections skipped over, beginning with Bad weather. [TGH]

32. Comparison with other manuscripts, and the lack of a paired line for this couplet, suggests a line has been omitted. In MS T, this reads: Tu seras tenu de teste fou. []

33. Most manuscripts read graciouse here; this variant in MS 7 is interesting as it alters the sense, reorienting this part of the line to reinforce the earlier word sale rather than offering the surprising juxtaposition of other MSS (which then produces a sustained contrast with the subsequently juxtaposed blaunche nape and megre table two lines later). [TGH]

34. At this point, MS 7 proceeds to the section Bad Weather omitted higher up. [TGH]

35. The scribe appears not to have understood the reference to Alienor here, resulting in a mangled reading. [TGH]

36. The text breaks off at this point. [TGH]
